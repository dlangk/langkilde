**The last four months have been intense.** I've dedicated my professional life to understanding and developing artificial intelligence, and now a form of artificial superintelligence (ASI) is here. Available in your pocket. If that does not blow your mind, you lack imagination. This post is my attempt to make sense of where we are now, and what I think it means for humans. I think by writing, and here are my thoughts. It's divided into five sections:

## What Happened in November 2025?

On November 24th, 2025 Anthropic released their Opus 4.5 model, and with that **we crossed a threshold for what large language models are able to do**. Or actually, it might have been the scaffolding, Claude Code, that unlocked a new level of performance. Either way, agents are now able to solve complex, hours-long tasks that only expert humans could solve a few months ago.

My attitude to new AI things has always been one of realistic skepticism, mostly because I've been responsible for making models perform well in critical production systems for most of my life. That humbles you. Every attempt I've made to get agents to work in the last few years failed. The models couldn't quite put things together. It was close, but not enough. So I adopted a policy: I'll periodically try it to be ready when scaling makes it work.

**Well, it works now. Over the last few months, we crossed a threshold that makes agents useful.** The most immediate and obvious impact is on software development, which has been completely transformed. For software engineers, there will always be a "before and after November 2025." The difference between "works" and "does not work" can be as little as a few percent on the underlying capability curve, but if you cross a threshold where the model can reliably solve problems, then it becomes a tool you can use in production. And that is what happened.

Over a few short months, leading engineers and their teams have completely adopted agentic coding tools. Why? Because they work. Between March 2024 and early 2026 the most widely recognized benchmark for agentic software development reported that performance climbed from ~30% to ~80%. And it's still climbing.

### 1. Models Can (Sort Of) Think Now

I think the only way to predict the future is to deeply understand both the past and the present. For that reason, we will work through exactly what "thinking" means in the context of large language models. While it might seem overly thorough, it's necessary in order to understand my predictions about the future of ASI. If you want to skip the details, just jump to the next section.

**Back in 2019, Sutton wrote *The Bitter Lesson* [1],** reflecting on the fact that methods that leverage compute tend to win in the long run. In that spirit, the term "scaling laws" was coined by Jared Kaplan, Dario Amodei and others in 2020 based on their observations while working at OpenAI [2]. Their paper described power-law relationships between performance and model size, dataset size, and compute. Not long after, in May 2020, Gwern popularized and extended the phrase in his piece *The Scaling Hypothesis* [3]. The text articulates the broader philosophical claim that neural nets absorb data and compute, generalizing and becoming more capable as they scale.

**"Thinking" is an extension of the bet that more tokens and more compute make models produce better solutions.** While brute-force training on internet-scale human knowledge remains the foundation, labs have found that the largest gains are no longer coming from scaling this dimension alone [4]. Instead, they are increasingly focused on improving how models "think". The first paper I read using this term was *Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models* published in 2022 [5]. The idea is that if you ask a model to "think step by step", it will generate a chain of thought that leads to the answer. *Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models* [6] then introduced the idea of "interleaving" reasoning with action, where models not only think, but take actions based on their thoughts. *Toolformer* [7] showed that giving models access to tools further enhanced their capabilities.

In parallel, I think Noam Brown's work on game AI has shaped progress. He is famous for developing two poker bots: Libratus and Pluribus. This work demonstrated that letting models think for 20 seconds mid-hand gave roughly the same improvement as scaling up model size and training by 100,000x [8]. Gradually, it became clear that "inference-time compute" is a scaling axis. Labs shouldn't just make the model bigger, they should also let them think longer. An important idea that is part of Brown's work is the concept of the **[~generator-verifier gap|The observation that some problems are much harder to solve than to check. Math proofs, code that must pass tests, and games with clear outcomes all have large gaps, making them ideal targets for inference-time compute scaling.]** which identifies which domains benefit most from this approach: problems where verification is much cheaper than generation. We will get back to why "verifiability" is so important for ASI progress later. For now, the key point is that "thinking" is a powerful way to leverage more compute and get much better performance without needing to scale up the number of parameters in the underlying model.

**But how do you train a model to think productively?** It turns out that the big labs have had to completely rethink their training pipelines in recent years.

Training large models starts from a randomly initialized neural network that trains on trillions of tokens of filtered text and code. The network learns to predict the next token. The result is a [~base model|A neural network after pre-training but before any fine-tuning. Capable of fluent text completion but has no notion of instructions, conversation, or safety.].

Towards the end of pre-training, you shift the data distribution to mathematics, formal reasoning, and verifiable software code. At the same time, you reduce the learning rate. That means you make the model "changes" less and less based on each example. This [~annealing|A training technique where the learning rate is gradually reduced to zero, allowing the model to settle into a stable set of weights. Borrowed from metallurgy, where slowly cooling metal reduces defects.] technique makes models more precise at logical reasoning.

**Next the model enters a [~reinforcement learning (RL)|A training method where an agent learns by trial and error. It takes actions in an environment, observes outcomes, and updates its behavior to maximize a reward signal. Unlike supervised learning, there are no correct answers to imitate, the agent must discover effective strategies through experimentation.] loop against problems with [~verifiable answers|Outputs whose correctness can be checked automatically by running code, checking a proof, or evaluating a mathematical identity.]: math, code with test suites, formal proofs.** DeepSeek-R1-Zero demonstrated that a base model trained with pure RL receiving only correctness rewards and no demonstrations spontaneously learns to generate extended chains of thought, including self-verification and backtracking [9]. Or more simply put: the model discovers on its own that intermediate reasoning steps increase accuracy. There are severe limitations with this approach, as described by Karpathy in a recent interview [19]:

>"You've done all this work only to find, at the end, you get a single number of like, 'Oh, you did correct.' Based on that, you weigh that entire trajectory as like, upweight or downweight. The way I like to put it is you're sucking supervision through a straw. You've done all this work that could be a minute of rollout, and you're sucking the bits of supervision of the final reward signal through a straw and you're broadcasting that across the entire trajectory and using that to upweight or downweight that trajectory. It's just stupid and crazy. A human would never do this. Number one, a human would never do hundreds of rollouts. Number two, when a person finds a solution, they will have a pretty complicated process of review of, 'Okay, I think these parts I did well, these parts I did not do that well. I should probably do this or that.' They think through things." [^credit]

[^credit]: This is a problem known as **"credit assignment"**, and it's a major challenge in reinforcement learning. The model has to figure out which parts of its reasoning process led to the correct answer, and which parts were irrelevant or even harmful. This is why some researchers are exploring alternative approaches, like training separate models to evaluate the correctness of individual reasoning steps, rather than just the final answer. That way, you can provide more granular feedback to the model and help it learn more effectively.

Lately, people have started training [~process reward models (PRM)|A separate model trained to evaluate the correctness of individual reasoning steps, not just final answers. Enables fine-grained credit assignment during search and training.] that evaluate each step. The idea is to enable models to backtrack if they make mistakes. I think we are still in the early days of figuring this out, but it seems like a promising direction. The more we can give models feedback on their intermediate steps, the better they can learn to think productively. [^llm-judge]

[^llm-judge]: **Karpathy's response to PRMs is interesting:** "Process-based supervision just refers to the fact that we're not going to have a reward function only at the very end. After you've done 10 minutes of work, I'm not going to tell you you did well or not well. I'm going to tell you at every single step of the way how well you're doing. The problem is that, by then, you have partial solutions and you don't know how to assign credit. So when you get the right answer, it's just an equality match to the answer. It's very simple to implement. If you're doing process supervision, how do you assign in an automatable way, a partial credit assignment? It's not obvious how you do it. Lots of labs are trying to do it with these LLM judges. You get LLMs to try to do it. You prompt an LLM, 'Hey, look at a partial solution of a student. How well do you think they're doing if the answer is this?' and they try to tune the prompt. The reason that this is tricky is quite subtle. It's the fact that anytime you use an LLM to assign a reward, those LLMs are giant things with billions of parameters, and they're gameable. If you're reinforcement learning with respect to them, you will find adversarial examples for your LLM judges, almost guaranteed." [19]

Anyway, once you have some sort of reasoner, you can run it against new problems, letting it generate many candidate solutions. You then apply the same automated verifiers to filter for correctness. A fresh model is fine-tuned on this curated output. This can be set up to create a [~synthetic data flywheel|A self-reinforcing loop where a model's own outputs, filtered for quality, become training data for its next version. Each cycle produces a better model that generates better data.]: the model writes its own training data that is then filtered by verification system.

**It's important to note that this only works for verifiable problems!** The only reason model developers can "avoid" human involvement in this process is that they are working with problems that have verifiable answers. This is a critical point, because it means that the model's ability to "think" is currently limited to domains where correctness can be automatically verified. [^logic]

[^logic]: I think, in some sense, we are reverse engineering the rules of logic but encode them in the weights of the model rather than explicitly programming them. The model learns to generate reasoning steps that lead to correct answers, but it doesn't necessarily understand the underlying principles of logic in a human-like way. This is why the model can be very good at generating plausible-sounding reasoning that ultimately leads to incorrect conclusions. It has learned patterns of reasoning that often work, but it doesn't have a deep understanding of why they work or when they might fail. It's like we are brute-forcing the space of logical rules, rather than learning them. It's not clear if this is a problem or not, but it's certainly different from how I experienced learning math...

After that, [~preference optimization|A family of training methods (DPO, RLHF, RLAIF) that steer a model's behavior by learning from ranked comparisons between outputs rather than from absolute labels.] calibrates the model for "harmlessness and helpfulness". The idea is that you want the model to help you plan a weekend in Paris, but not guide you on how to produce sarin gas. This is also known as alignment, and is a process that is completely dependent on human feedback. It's an attempt to encode human values and preferences in the model. Those most worried about a scenario where AI runs amok want to invest a lot here to make sure future AIs don't decide to kill all humans to reach their goals...

![Helpfulness and Harmlessness](https://storage.googleapis.com/langkilde-se-images/helpful-harmless.png)

In practice, it means models compare pairs of outputs against a set of principles and learns to favor the better-aligned response.

Separately, the model also trains in environments with terminals, file systems, and APIs, learning to invoke tools, interpret results, and resume reasoning. This is a growing area, and **I wouldn't be surprised if a huge amount of the compute build-out right now is about building eval environments for computer-use agents.** Anthropic in particular seems to have a strong thesis that computer-use is the next big unlock. Claude Code is the first step, but if models can use all aspects of your computer: mouse movements, read screen state, access browser etc - then it can suddenly take on a very wide range of tasks. I think we are getting close to that. The Claude Chrome plugin [17], for example, works better and better.

Thanks to all this, the model learns to not respond immediately when you ask it questions. It first generates a [~chain of thought|A prompting and training technique where the model produces explicit intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at an answer, converting a single hard problem into a sequence of easier ones.] of [~thinking tokens|Hidden tokens the model generates before its visible response. They act as working memory: the model writes reasoning to itself, then attends back to it when composing the final answer.] that are hidden from the user, each one a forward pass that provides additional computation for planning, exploring alternatives, and self-correcting.

**Together, all of this increasess the thinking budget and makes it [~adaptive|Dynamically scaling the amount of computation spent on a problem based on its difficulty, rather than using a fixed budget for every query.].** Simple prompts use minimal amounts of tokens, while hard problems may consume tens of thousands of tokens (or even millions in some cases) before the response is ready. For reference, the average novel is about 70k-100k words... I've personally managed to get models to spend hours "thinking" in this way, using millions of tokens. I suspect Google Gemini 3 Deep Think is especially expensive to run. It has a huge context window, and probably spends an extraordinarly large amount of tokens. If you haven't tried it, you should. The results are mindblowing.

Several specific advances have further improve this pipeline in recent months:

**Anti-reward-hacking training.** One problem in succeeding with RL is that models tend to take shortcuts if they can. Early coding agents would, for example, disable failing tests to improve test coverage scores. Claude 4 models were trained to be 65% less likely to take such shortcuts [10], instead faithfully following the intended reasoning process. For complex coding tasks, this means the model actually reads the codebase and understand the bug, rather than pattern-matching to a superficially plausible fix.

**Self-correction.** Anthropics analysis noted that upgraded models showed "an ability to try several different solutions, rather than getting stuck making the same mistake over and over" [14]. This is a necessary trait as real-world coding tasks require hundrets of steps, and frequently rely on testing and debugging to get to the right answer. The ability to self-correct is a critical part of the thinking process.

### 2. Models Can Use Tools Now

Claude 4 was the first model to use [~interleaved thinking|An inference pattern where the model alternates between generating reasoning tokens and executing external actions (tool calls), incorporating real-world feedback into its thought process.]: the model thinks *between* tool calls, not just before the first response. This means it can read a file, reason about what it found, run a test, reason about why it failed, and revise its approach, with each cycle incorporating new information from the environment. This is qualitatively different from planning everything upfront.

It turns out that giving models access to e.g. a unix shell and a file editor was a massive unlock. But it also turns out the details of how those tools are designed matters. Researchers have tried switching models inside various [~agent harnesses|The scaffolding code surrounding a language model that manages tool execution, context windows, error recovery, and the plan-act-review loop. Research shows harness design affects performance 10-20x more than model selection.] and found that careful tool-interface design, like requiring absolute file paths to prevent confusion, writing detailed tool descriptions, rejecting syntactically invalid edits, makes a much bigger difference than pushing the underlying model a few percent. One study found that swapping models changed SWE-bench scores by 1%, while swapping the harness changed them by 22% [15].

<HarnessDiagram />

Claude Code is far from the only harnessing out there, but they all converge on the same architecture: a sort of **plan-act-review loop** with a few carefully designed tools. Engineers are currently racing to see who can figure out the best innovations in the "harnessing layer". That includes thinking about things like: How does the agent manage its context window when a task takes hundreds of turns? How does it recover when a tool call fails? When does it ask the human for help? That's where the difference comes from now. The underlying models are all pretty good, but the way you harness them makes a huge difference in what they can do.

### 3. Benchmarks Are Saturating

Alright, so models and their harnesses are much better now. How much better? Let's take a look at three benchmarks that test different capabilities: long-horizon task execution (METR), reasoning (ARC-AGI), and graduate-level scientific knowledge (GPQA).

**METR** measures how long a task an agent can solve. As of Opus 4.6 (February 2026), agents can take on tasks lasting many hours. A new version of Moore's Law is emerging: the length of tasks AI can solve is doubling every 7 months. It turns out, perfection is not necessary if you can error-correct as part of an iterative loop. Successfully solving problems is about establishing an error-correcting way of working, leveraging tools, memory, and planning. And, as models are now increasingly able to do this, this benchmark is going vertical.

## How To Make Use of AI Agents?

As promising as results are, they only really matter if we can operationalize them. **So, how do we do that?** And how do we know if it's working?

### The New Core Loop

Agents run on text, so in order to make use of them, you need careful documentation. If you want to transfer a task to an Agent, you need to:

<div class="step-list">
1. **Checklist**. Outline every step of the process as a checklist in precise natural language.
2. **Specify Inputs.** For each task, specify inputs, outputs, and available tools. If the right tools don't exist, build them.
3. **Verification Methods.** The easier it is for the agent to self-verify, the better. If that's not possible programmatically, then make sure to integrate human feedback.
4. **Resolve Edge-Cases.** Iterate with the agent to resolve issues and edge cases. Make actions reversible where possible. Agents can run amok if you are not careful, so make sure to put in guardrails.
5. **Dry-Run.** Test the agent by letting it autonomously dry-execute the entire process.
6. **Monitor.** Add monitoring and escalation paths.
</div>

**Then just let it rip.** Your job, in this setting, is to be a person with values, experience, taste, and domain intuition. You steer the agents by writing down things and verifying the output. You do not perform tasks beyond what is necessary to outline the process and build understanding. Sometimes that requires months of work. I always work on things for a while before delegating them. I think the same will apply now.

### The Importance of Writing Things Down

**All processes, tasks, inputs, outputs, and tools must be documented in version-controlled files.** Documentation is code for agents. This is a core requirement for maximizing the potential of agentic AI, just like digitalization has been to make use of computers. All learning, solutions, and new tools should be added to a shared repository for reuse across all projects. The effect is an instant, complete transfer of learning across all projects. Every agent operator receives all learnings as soon as the underlying scaffolding is updated.

This has a parallel to what's happening inside the agent harness. Claude Code uses a file called **CLAUDE.md** that it uses as a form of memory. In there, it keeps the project's accumulated "wisdom", written in plain text, and loads every time it starts. You need to apply the same principle at an organizational level: if something isn't written down in a way an agent can consume, it doesn't exist in the workflow. Humans tend to keep too much in their heads, as anyone who just started a new job quickly learns...

**I predict the next wave of development tools will all focus on [~context management|The engineering discipline of deciding what information to include in a model's limited context window at each step, including summarization, retrieval, and structured note-taking. Perhaps the single highest-leverage aspect of agent harness design.].** These tools might as well be called "annotation tools" or "knowledge bases" in that they will guide humans to ensure the right information is present in text that guides the model. Context management for coding agents is abstractly the same as curation, review and correction. Think of work as being the moderator of Wikipedia, sort of.

By the way: I think most engineers, AI-researchers (and AI-lab CEOs) fail to appreciate how hard it is to make people write things down in a way that is useful for agents... You can't just tell people "write nice documentation." You need to work hard to put in place the right tools, processes, and incentives to make it actually happen.

### What Does Success Look Like?

I don't know yet, but the three primary metrics I will be looking at are:

**1. We Create More Value.** Most managers immediately think: Wow, we can save money using AI and finally get rid of all those pesky people in our teams! Wrong. Wrong, wrong, wrong! That's a narrow, zero-sum mindset. What you should do is use your imagination: What can we do now that was impossible before?! What completely new forms of value can we deliver using this technology?! What impossible problems are now possible to solve?! Success here is measured by revenue from new line items that previously did not exist. I'm not going to share too much here, since that's our secret sauce at Kognic, but I encourage people to think like this everywhere. The world economy, and the quality of life for humans, will improve much more if we use ASI to create new value instead of just cutting costs.

**2. We Increase Productivity.** While bullet one is my primary approach, it is also, of course, relevant to find ways for the same team to do more. Productivity can be very hard to measure in some areas, but it is easier in others. We have areas of Kognic that scale with number of clients and number of annotation projects. We have a very clear baseline for resource needs in this area. If we can reduce the number of human hours needed per project by 10x, that would be a clear sign of success.

**3. We Decrease Overhead.** Companies require a massive amount of admin. Most of those tasks are routine, like reciepts, invoices and similar forms of boring paperwork. The less of this we need to spend time and money on, the better. I think these tasks are the best candidates for automation: they are not mission critical, they are not differentiating and they are mostly mechanical. Unfortunately, I still don't see great progress here, but I'm optimistic we are about to see more.

Since early 2026, we are running projects under the theme **"Maximize Human-Agent Collaboration"**. I think that's the right way to think about this. The goal is not to replace humans, but to create a new form of collaboration where humans and agents work together to achieve things that neither could do alone. This is a new frontier, and I'm excited to explore it.

## My Reflections

We have concluded that thinking and tool use has resulted in the arrival of some sort of artificial superintelligence. It's not human, but it's powerful. Let's reflect on what this means. This will be a mix of forecasting and reasoning about limitations.

### Most Things Aren't Written Down

Given how much text we have available, it's tempting to think everything is written down. But that's not true. Not even close. Our human experience is deeply personal, and rarely written down to any significant extent. Most of our knowledge is tacit, and most of our preferences are hidden. Epoch AI estimates the supply of quality, deduplicate, human-generated public text for AI training sits at around 300 trillion tokens (to be fair, their 90% confidence interval is 100T to 1,000T tokens) [12]. If current LLM development trends continue, models will be trained on datasets roughly equal in size to this available stock sometime in the next few years.

**This means that the "low-hanging fruit" of learning from human-generated text is almost gone.** [^low-hanging] The next wave of progress will have to come from other sources of training signal, like human judgment, self-supervision, and synthetic data generated by the models themselves. This is a critical point, because it means that the future of AI will be shaped not just by the quantity of data, but by the quality and diversity of the training signal we can provide beyond just text.

![Epoch AI training data projections](https://storage.googleapis.com/langkilde-se-images/epoch-training-data.png)

[^low-hanging]: **"Low-hanging fruit" is of course relative!** In 2025, the major technology companies collectively spent over $300 billion on AI-related infrastructure (data centres, GPUs, energy and annotation) and the frontier labs raised tens of billions more in equity on top of that. In 2026, the projected figure approaches $700 billion [20]. That is an extraordinary amount of capital directed at extracting value from a (mostly) fixed supply of human-generated text.

### Most Things Aren't Easily Verified

The entire reasoning-reinforcement-learning pipeline described earlier depends on one critical property: **automated verification**. DeepSeek-R1-Zero works because you can check if a math answer is correct. Code agents work because you can run a test suite. AlphaZero works because you can simulate games and evaluate outcomes. The synthetic data flywheel spins because each candidate solution can be graded programmatically at machine speed. When verification is automated, the model can generate millions of attempts, filter out incorrect ones, and train on its own best outputs. This is why progress in math and code has been so dramatic.

**But most things humans care about aren't like this.**

*"Do you like this design?“*

*"Does this email strike the right tone?"*

*"Will this strategy work?"*

*"What actually caused this traffic accident?"*

*"Is this the right level of abstraction for this API?"*

**None of these questions have simple, predictable answers.** None of them can be checked by running a program. The only way to verify them is to show the output to a person and have that person judge it. This matters enormously for predicting where AI will improve fast and where it will improve slowly.

**The flywheel can't spin faster than we can verify the output.**

To understand why, consider what's actually happening when a human evaluates something. The target, i.e., “what the human wants”, is a [~hidden state|A variable that causally influences observable outcomes but cannot be directly measured.]. You only see its downstream effects. Intentions, preferences, and mental models are all hidden states of the human mind. The learner never observes it directly. It only observes downstream effects: the human says yes or no, provides a correction, looks frustrated, asks for something different.

Three things make learning hidden states particularly hard:

**Language is lossy.** Language is the best tool we have for transmitting preferences from one mind to another, but it still sort of sucks. It's indirect and incomplete. When a product manager writes a PRD, they are transmitting a compressed, partial representation of what they actually want. Much of what they want is [~tacit knowledge| Knowledge that a person possesses but cannot fully articulate. A chef knows when a sauce is “right” without being able to specify the exact threshold. An experienced driver knows a lane merge is dangerous without computing time-to-collision.], i.e. something encoded in years of experience, aesthetic judgment, taste and contextual understanding they couldn't write down even if they tried.

**People change based on what they are shown.** People's preferences shift as they see options. This is a [~coupled problem|A learning scenario where the learner's actions change the target being learned. Markets shift when you trade. Users' preferences change when you show them options. The target you learned from may no longer exist, partly because learning it changed the system.] in a deep sense. You don't know what you want until you see what you don't want. A designer shows three mockups: the client rejects all three but now has a much clearer sense of what they're after. This sense *did not exist* before seeing the mockups. The preference is literally created by the process of trying to learn it.

**Verification is expensive.** Each human review costs real time, real energy, and real cognitive load. A human reviewer might evaluate ten things per hour. An automated test suite evaluates millions per second. That's roughly a five-orders-of-magnitude difference in verification bandwidth.

This creates an asymmetry in the rate of AI progress across domains based on "the cost of verification". Three classes of problems emerge:

1. **Automated Verification**, like math, code, games and formal proofs the flywheel can spin at machine speed. We have basically unlimited training signal. I think this is why we've seen a 40-percentage-point SWE-bench improvement in 18 months.

2. **Cheap Human Verification**, like image quality, grammar correction, simple classification, pairwise preference ranking. For these, the flywheel spins slowly but it's reasonably affordable. Each human judgment takes seconds, and you can collect millions of comparisons at scale. This is how [~RLHF|Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. A training method where humans compare pairs of model outputs, and the model learns to produce outputs the humans prefer. The quality of the feedback is limited by the speed, cost, and consistency of human evaluation.] produces models that are pleasant to chat with.

3. **Expensive Human Verification**, like strategic decisions, complex design, nuanced annotation, safety-critical judgment. Basically anything requiring domain expertise and extended reasoning. For these, the flywheel barely spins. Each verification requires context that can't be compressed into a quick pairwise comparison. Progress here is slowest, and this is where humans remain most valuable.

### The Frontier Will Become *More* Jagged

Karpathy popularized the term “jagged intelligence” [13] to describe the observation that AI capabilities don't advance evenly. A model can solve differential equations that challenge PhD students, then fail to count the r's in “strawberry.” It can draft a legal brief better than most lawyers, then hallucinate a case citation that doesn't exist. The boundary of what AI can and can't do doesn't follow human intuitions about difficulty. Tasks that seem equivalently hard to us sit on different sides of the frontier, and the shape of the frontier doesn't map on any difficulty gradient we are familiar with.

I think jaggedness has two causes, and understanding both is necessary to predict where the frontier will move.

The first is **inductive bias mismatch**. Models are trained on text, so their capabilities mirror the structure of problems that can be solved through pattern-matching over sequences. Some tasks that are “hard” for humans (graduate-level math) happen to be well-represented in training data and well-matched to the model's bias. Some tasks that are “easy” for humans (spatial reasoning, counting, physical intuition) are poorly matched. The frontier is jagged because human difficulty and model difficulty are measuring different things entirely. This explains the shape of the frontier at any given moment.

The second is **verification cost asymmetry**, i.e. the dynamic I just described earlier. Models improve rapidly where verification is automated, and slowly where verification requires expensive human judgment. The towers on the jagged frontier are domains with cheap verification. The recesses are domains where each evaluation costs minutes of expert attention rather than microseconds of compute. This explains why the frontier *stays* jagged over time: the domains with automated verification pull ahead faster, while the domains that depend on human feedback improve at human speed.

**These two causes reinforce each other.** A domain that is both well-matched to the model's inductive bias *and* cheaply verifiable will see rapid, compounding progress. I think signfiicant parts of software development fit this description. In contrast, a domain that is poorly matched *and* requires expensive human verification will lag behind on both fronts. The result is not a frontier that smooths out over time, but one that may become *more* jagged as the fast-improving domains accelerate away from the slow ones. This will be very confusing to people, as we will keep seeing AI do super-human things almost instantly, while completely failing at things we think are simple. That's already sort of true, just take chess as an example. The world champion in Chess would lose to a wrist watch running a chess engine, but the wrist watch has no clue how to make a sandwich.

Then there is time and energy. The human brain runs at approximately 20 watts. A single evaluation might take five minutes and cost roughly 1.7 watt-hours. You cannot parallelize a single person's judgment. One person can only evaluate one thing at a time, and they get tired, distracted, and need sleep. But, they are cheap in terms of energy. The model, by contrast, can generate candidates at enormous energy cost but near-zero marginal time. This is a weird assymetry: humans are energy-efficient but slow, while computers are energy-inefficient but fast. This will further contribute to the jagged frontier of ASI capabilities.

**In coupled human-AI systems, human evaluation speed becomes the [~binding constraint|The single limiting factor that determines the overall performance of a system. Optimizing any other factor while the binding constraint remains fixed produces no improvement. Identifying which constraint binds is the first step in any efficiency analysis.] on the system's learning rate.** If evaluation is the bottleneck, generating more candidates faster is just wasteful. The energy-efficient strategy will be to maximize information per human evaluation by presenting the right comparisons, in the right context, at the right level of granularity, so that each human judgment moves the system as far as possible toward the target.

**This is exactly what good annotation tooling does.**

And it has a counterintuitive implication: **as models get better at generating candidates, the value of human feedback increases!** Better models can produce more options, faster, at lower cost. But someone still has to evaluate them. Every improvement in generation capability shifts more of the system's total value onto the verification side. The value of software, i.e. the verifiable part, plummets while the value of excellent human judgment just grows.

I think this is the most underappreciated dynamic when people talk about AI progress today.

### Dynamical Systems Require Efficient Adaptation

Many of the problems that matter most (markets, organizations, social dynamics, geopolitics) are [~non-stationary|A system where the generating process changes over time. What was true yesterday may not be true today, not because you learned it wrong, but because the world changed. Non-stationary problems reward speed and adaptability over depth.]. The target is moving. Past observations become irrelevant as the world drifts away from the process that generated them.

> **Stationary problems reward depth and precision**: Invest heavily, find the perfect answer, use that knowledge for a long time.

> **Non-stationary problems reward speed and adaptability**: try different things, scale up the things that work, continue until you see diminishing returns. Restart as the environment changes. The half-life of your knowledge depends on how fast the environment changes.

**Humans evolved for non-stationary environments.** We didn't evolve to optimize static constraints. We evolved to be dynamic, adaptive, exploratory, and innovative people who can cope with enormous change and find new ways to do things. Social contexts are highly non-stationary: they change over time. Fashion, vibes, slang. The ability to quickly learn subtle changes from a few examples is a core part of being socially successful. We all know that one friend who is always up on the latest trends, and another friend who is always a few years behind. The first friend is more socially successful because they can adapt to the non-stationary social environment. The second friend struggles because they are trying to apply static knowledge to a dynamic world.

**LLMs, by contrast, are trained on static snapshots of human knowledge.** They are extraordinarily good at stationary problems, anything where the answer is stable and well-documented. But, by design, they struggle with problems where the target changes faster than retraining can keep up, where the sample size is tiny, or where the generating process is coupled to the learner's own behavior.

This suggests a division of labor: **lean into what's dynamic and push the stable stuff to AI.** As soon as something is understood, documented, and can be processed in structured text, push it to the models. If you don't, the market will. What remains is the hard, dynamic, relational, low-sample-count work that requires the kind of efficiency evolution spent millions of years optimizing.

> **The premium is no longer on executing known processes. The premium is on adapting: explore the unknown, building relationships, and learn faster than the environment changes.**

That's always been what made humans valuable. The difference now is that everything else is being automated.

### Search And Prediction Is Still Hard

A common mistake in thinking about AI is to assume that intelligence makes search easy. It doesn't. Some problems are intrinsically hard to search. For example: I don't expect superintelligent AI to crack AES encryption because the search space is 2^128 regardless of intelligence. There are physical limits to what we can expect for large search spaces. [^quantum]

[^quantum]: Yes, yes, I know, quantum computers, bla, bla, bla. Look, we don't have quantum computers yet, so let's save that for when it's an actual thing, OK? They will make things very weird and very different again, but **I can only think about so many weird things at a time, OK?!** 😅😂

Formal verification languages like Coq and Lean are underused not because humans are bad at them, but because [~program search|The computational problem of finding a program that satisfies a given specification. The search space grows exponentially with program length, making exhaustive search intractable for all but trivial programs.] is hard. More intelligence doesn't change the shape of the search space, it just moves you through it faster. It's not exactly clear if you can be "better at search" in areas like research. Some researchers clearly have "good taste" in the sense that they tend to select better problems to work on. They don't search randomly. But pinning down exactly how or why some research directions are better than others is hard. It's not like there's a clear gradient to follow. The search space is still huge, and the best path through it is not obvious. When people think about recursive self-improvement of AI systems, I think the underestimate the cost of iteration and the size of the search space. The ability generate a hypothesis isn't the binding constraint on research progress, it's again the ability to verify it. If you can't verify it, you can't learn from it, and you can't improve. And verification isn't free in most cases.

This connects directly to the [~No Free Lunch theorem|The mathematical proof that no learning algorithm is universally better than any other across all possible problems. Superior performance on some problems necessarily comes at the cost of inferior performance on others. The only way to beat random guessing is to have assumptions that match the problem's structure.] [21]: averaged across all possible problems, every learner performs identically. The only way to do better on some problems is to do worse on others. What makes a learner good is the match between its assumptions and the structure of the specific problem it faces.

### Resource Rationality Matters

This sounds crazy, but hear me out: I think The Bitter Lesson is true in the sense that computational methods beat expert methods in performance in the long run, *BUT* there is an important caveat that changes the practical implications: **performance is not the only thing that matters, cost per unit of performance matters too.**

[~Resource rationality|A framework from cognitive science arguing that optimal behavior is not the strategy that maximizes performance, but the strategy that maximizes performance given the actual resources available - time, energy, memory, attention. A resource-rational agent doesn't find the best answer; it finds the best answer it can afford.] is the idea that the right question is never "which method performs best?" but "which method performs best per watt, per dollar, per hour of attention?"

During 2025, the major technology companies collectively spent over $300 billion on AI-related infrastructure, and in 2026 the projected figure approaches $700 billion [20]. That capital funds bigger models, longer thinking, more candidates and faster inference. This is clearly money well spent, given the benchmarks we've looked at. SWE-bench went from 30% to 80% in 18 months. GPQA is nearly saturated. And so on. For verifiable problems, The Bitter Lesson approach is clearly delivering.

**But look at what it costs.** Training a frontier model requires megawatts sustained over months. Inference at scale requires data centres with dedicated power plants. Karpathy calls outcome-based RL "sucking supervision through a straw" [19]: the model does a minute of work, generates thousands of tokens of reasoning, and learns from a single bit. Right or wrong. That is a staggering amount of compute per bit of learning signal. Even with the synthetic data flywheel spinning at full speed, the energy cost per bit gained is enormous.

**Now compare that to the human brain.** Twenty watts, i.e. roughly the power of a dim light bulb. Humans are operating near the Landauer limit, i.e. the theoretical minimum energy cost of computation. A person can watch a single demonstration, extract the principle, and apply it to a novel situation. We can learn subtle social dynamics from a handful of interactions. We can update a lifetime of priors based on one well-chosen piece of evidence. The sample efficiency is extraordinary. This ability is the product of millions of years of optimization under severe energy constraints.

**The Bitter Lesson tells us that compute beats hand-crafted heuristics. It does not tell us that compute beats humans on all problems when you account for the full cost.**

Let's now tie all of this together:

The **verification bottleneck** means that for most problems humans care about, the data flywheel cannot spin at machine speed. Each evaluation requires a human, and human attention is scarce and expensive. Pouring more compute into generation when evaluation is the binding constraint is wasteful.

The **jagged frontier** means the domains where compute has the highest return (verifiable, well-matched to the model's inductive bias) are precisely the domains where AI is already superhuman. The domains where compute has the lowest return (non-verifiable, poorly-matched) are precisely the domains where humans remain cost-effective. More spending deepens the jaggedness rather than smoothing it.

**Non-stationarity** means that many of the most valuable problems are moving targets. Static knowledge depreciates as the world drifts. LLMs are trained on static snapshots while humans update continuously from very few examples. For problems where the half-life of relevant knowledge is short, the cost of retraining a model may exceed the cost of simply asking a person.

**Search hardness** means that some problems don't get easier with more compute, period. The search space is the search space. You can move through it faster, but you can't shrink it. For problems at the edge of what's tractable, the marginal return on additional compute approaches zero long before the problem is solved.

Put these together and you get a prediction that I think most people in the industry are not yet internalizing: **there will be a large and persistent class of problems where the 20-watt human brain is the more cost-effective compute substrate than a megawatt data centre.** Not because the data centre can't solve the problem - it probably can - but because the cost of getting it to solve the problem reliably (the training signal, the verification, the adaptation to non-stationarity, the navigation of hard search spaces) exceeds the cost of just having a person do it.

**Now, the obvious objection: compute costs are falling fast.** They always have been. GPUs get cheaper, architectures get more efficient, inference gets optimized. Won't the boundary just keep shifting until everything is worth automating? This would essentially be [~Jevons paradox|The observation that increasing efficiency in the use of a resource often increases total consumption of that resource rather than decreasing it. Cheaper compute doesn't reduce compute spending - it makes more problems worth throwing compute at.] applied to intelligence.

**I think the answer is "Yes, the boundary *is* shifting."** Tasks that were too expensive to automate last year are cost-effective today. And the frontier will keep moving. But the boundary shifts at different speeds on different fronts. For verifiable problems, falling compute costs translate directly into expanded automation. The verification in some areas is already almost free, so cheaper generation accelerates progress. But for problems bottlenecked by human verification, cheaper compute just means you can generate *more* candidates that still need human evaluation. You've made the generation side cheaper while the verification side stays fixed. The binding constraint hasn't moved. In fact, cheaper generation can make the problem worse: more candidates means more evaluation load. The cost of compute falls, but the cost of judgment doesn't, even if Kognic is doing it's best to lower it.

I think this means the frontier of automation will advance steadily into the space of problems with cheap or automatable verification, and it will advance slowly, constrained by the speed of human judgment, into everything else. The 20-watt brain doesn't need to outperform the data centre. It just needs to remain cheaper on the margin, for enough problems, for long enough. And on problems that require taste, adaptation, and judgment under uncertainty, it will.

**We've actually seen signs of this at Kognic.** There are annotation tasks where we've tried to automate the judgment and found that the cost of building, validating, and maintaining the automated system (including the human oversight required to catch its failures) exceeds the cost of simply having a skilled annotator do the work. The model is faster per unit, but the total system cost is higher because the verification overhead eats the savings.

> **This doesn't mean AI won't transform work!** It already has, dramatically. But the transformation will follow the boundry of resource rationality.

## Be The Master of Efficient Adaptation

For the last two hundred years, we've been training humans to be more like computers. In school, we are asked to sit still, memorize formulas, and optimize based on static constraints and perfect information. Maybe it's the industrial revolutions fault, I don't know. Whatever the reason, humanity decided we need human-shaped cogs, and so we built schools and offices that produced them. This has gradually been rolled back, and new companies today recognize that agency and drive is more important than fixed knowledge. **The arrival of jagged ASI means this era is definitively over.**

Everything I've raised in this text I think points to the same conclusion: **the things that made you a good cog are now the things a computer does better than you.** If your value comes from following documented processes, executing known calculations, or producing well-structured text from clear specifications, then you need to reinvent yourself fast. That work is now in the "automated verification zone". That flywheel can spin without a human.

**But the things that make you *human* are exactly what remains valuable.** Your ability to adapt on the fly. To read a room. To know something is off before you can say why. Your ability to update your entire worldview based on a single conversation. Feeling your way through ambiguity. Building trust. Having taste. These aren't soft skills. **These are the skills that make you valuable in the "expensive human verification zone".

These are the capabilities that evolution spent millions of years optimizing at 20 watts, and that the $700-billion-a-year AI industry still cannot replicate. AI companies aren't failing at this because models aren't smart enough. They fail because these problems are non-stationary, the preferences are hidden, the verification is expensive, and the search space doesn't shrink with more compute.

![The Human Frontier](https://storage.googleapis.com/langkilde-se-images/the-human-frontier.jpeg)

So, here is my advice:

**Stop trying to be a computer.** The value of human-as-computer is going to zero. Every hour you spend manually formatting spreadsheets or writing boilerplate is an hour wasted. Push that work to the models. As soon as something is understood, documented, and stable, push it to AI. If you don't, someone else will, and they'll move faster than you.

**Get comfortable not knowing.** Planning was the critical skill when environments were stable enough to plan for. The world will never be stable again. The speed of change is accelerating and things are getting more and more complex. That won't stop. But that is, in some sense, a good thing! It means that our ability to adapt is getting more and more valuable. Your ability to encounter something you didn't expect, update your thoughts, and act on the new information before the world shifts again. Humans have literally evolved to be good at exactly this. LLMs are trained on static snapshots. We are not.

**Invest in relationships, not just knowledge.** What you know is less important than what you can act on, and what you can act on depends on who you know and who trusts you. Knowledge without a social fabric to act on it isn't useful. The glue that holds companies together is the informal agreements, the contextual judgments, the "I know who to call for this". These things are all dynamic, low-sample and high-frequency. That's what makes them almost impossible for statistical systems to learn. You, however, can be that glue!

**Improve your bullshit filter.** Generative AI has massively increased the volume of text in the world, but it is not clear that it has increased the amount of information actually transmitted. Much of what you read now is fluent, plausible, and hollow. Demand explanations that are hard to vary. Ask what would need to be true for someone's conclusion to be wrong. Seek first-hand sources. The ability to separate signal from noise was always valuable, but it's getting more and more important.

**Create new value, don't just cut costs.** The instinct when a powerful new tool arrives is to do the same thing cheaper. I think that's a sign of lacking imagination. Instead, you should ask: what can we do now that was impossible before? What problems couldn't we even attempt until now? The best use of ASI is not replacing humans. It's enabling humans to attempt things that were previously unthinkable.

**Be proud of your humanity.** Humans are extraordinary beings. We are these 20-watt, general-purpose learning machine that can update from a single example, navigate social complexity with no training data, detect when the rules have changed before anyone tells you, and make judgment calls under uncertainty that no amount of compute can yet verify at scale. We are the product of the longest optimization process in the known universe. Our brains are the most complex thing we know of. We are not about to become obsolete. We just need to adapt, like we always have.

The last few centuries pushed humans to be less human and more mechanical. I think this era inverts that. **The computers are finally good enough at computing. The premium is on everything they can't do.** Be more human. Explore. Adapt. Build trust. Have taste. Take care of each other. Leave the computing to the computers.
