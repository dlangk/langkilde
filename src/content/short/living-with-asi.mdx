**The last four months have been intense.** I've dedicated my professional life to understanding and developing artificial intelligence, and now a form of artificial superintelligence (ASI) is here. Available in your pocket. If that does not blow your mind, you lack imagination. This post is my attempt to make sense of where we are now, and what I think it means for humans. I think by writing, and here are my thoughts:

## What Happened in November 2025?

On November 24th, 2025 Anthropic released their Opus 4.5 model, and with that **we crossed a threshold for what large language models are able to do**. Or actually, it might have been the scaffolding, Claude Code, that unlocked a new level of performance. Either way, agents are now able to solve complex, hours-long tasks that only expert humans could solve a few months ago.

My attitude to new AI things has always been one of realistic skepticism, mostly because I've been responsible for making models perform well in critical production systems for most of my life. That humbles you. Every attempt I've made to get agents to work in the last few years failed. The models couldn't quite put things together. It was close, but not enough. So I adopted a policy: I'll periodically try it to be ready when scaling makes it work.

**Well, it works now. Over the last few months, we crossed a threshold that makes agents useful.** The most immediate and obvious impact is on software development, which has been completely transformed. For software engineers, there will always be a "before and after November 2025." The difference between "works" and "does not work" can be as little as a few percent on the underlying capability curve, but if you cross a threshold where the model can reliably solve problems, then it becomes a tool you can use in production. And that is what happened.

Over a few short months, leading engineers and their teams have completely adopted agentic coding tools. Why? Because they work. Between March 2024 and early 2026 the most widely recognized benchmark for agentic software development reported that performance climbed from ~30% to ~80%. And it's still climbing.

**As mentioned, the increase in raw model performance with the release of Opus 4.5 isn't very big.** While the model release pushed us over the threshold, two things really boosted performance in recent months:

### 1. Models Can (Sort Of) Think Now

Sutton in 2019 wrote *The Bitter Lesson* [1], reflecting on the fact that methods that leverage compute tend to win in the long run. In that spirit, the term "scaling laws" was coined by Jared Kaplan, Dario Amodei and others in 2020 based on their observations while working at OpenAI [2]. Their paper described power-law relationships between performance and model size, dataset size, and compute. Not long after, in May 2020, Gwern popularized the phrase, and extended it, in his piece *The Scaling Hypothesis* [3]. He articulated the broader philosophical claim that neural nets absorb data and compute, generalizing and becoming more capable as they scale.

**"Thinking" is an extension of the bet that more tokens and more compute make models produce better solutions.** While brute-force training on internet-scale human knowledge remains the foundation, labs have found that the largest gains are no longer coming from scaling this dimension alone [4]. Instead, they are increasingly focused on improving how models "think". The first paper I read using this term was *Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models* published in 2022 [5]. The idea is that if you ask a model to "think step by step", it will generate a chain of thought that leads to the answer. *Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models* [6] then introduced the idea of "interleaving" reasoning with action, where models not only think, but take actions based on their thoughts. *Toolformer* [7] showed that giving models access to tools further enhanced their capabilities.

In parallel, Noam Brown's work on game AI provided the theoretical foundation for why this matters. His poker bots Libratus and Pluribus demonstrated that having a model think for 20 seconds mid-hand gave roughly the same improvement as scaling up model size and training by 100,000x [8]. Gradually, it became clear that "inference-time compute" is a scaling axis, i.e. don't just make the model bigger, also let it think longer. An important idea that is part of Brown's work is the concept of the **[~generator-verifier gap|The observation that some problems are much harder to solve than to check. Math proofs, code that must pass tests, and games with clear outcomes all have large gaps, making them ideal targets for inference-time compute scaling.]** which identifies which domains benefit most from this approach: problems where verification is much cheaper than generation. We will get back to why this insight is critical when we talk about the limitations of ASI later on. For now, the key point is that "thinking" is a powerful way to leverage more compute and get much better performance without needing to scale up the number of parameters in the underlying model as much.

**But how do you train a model to think productively?** It turns out that the big labs have had to completely rethink their training pipelines in recent years.

Training large models starts from a randomly initialized neural network that trains on trillions of tokens of filtered text and code. The network learns to predict the next token. The result is a [~base model|A neural network after pre-training but before any fine-tuning. Capable of fluent text completion but has no notion of instructions, conversation, or safety.].

Towards the end of pre-training, you shift the data distribution to mathematics, formal reasoning, and verifiable software code. At the same time, you reduce the learning rate. That means you make the model "changes" less and less based on each example. This [~annealing|A training technique where the learning rate is gradually reduced to zero, allowing the model to settle into a stable set of weights. Borrowed from metallurgy, where slowly cooling metal reduces defects.] technique makes models more precise at logical reasoning.

**Next the model enters a [~reinforcement learning (RL)|A training method where an agent learns by trial and error. It takes actions in an environment, observes outcomes, and updates its behavior to maximize a reward signal. Unlike supervised learning, there are no correct answers to imitate, the agent must discover effective strategies through experimentation.] loop against problems with [~verifiable answers|Outputs whose correctness can be checked automatically by running code, checking a proof, or evaluating a mathematical identity.]: math, code with test suites, formal proofs.** DeepSeek-R1-Zero demonstrated that a base model trained with pure RL receiving only correctness rewards and no demonstrations spontaneously learns to generate extended chains of thought, including self-verification and backtracking [9]. Or more simply put: the model discovers on its own that intermediate reasoning steps increase accuracy.

Once you have a reasoner, you run it against new problems, letting it generate many candidate solutions. You then apply the same automated verifiers to filter for correctness. A fresh model is fine-tuned on this curated output. This is the [~synthetic data flywheel|A self-reinforcing loop where a model's own outputs, filtered for quality, become training data for its next version. Each cycle produces a better model that generates better data.]: the model writes its own training data, filtered by verification. **It's important to note that this only works for verifiable problems.** The only reason model developers can "avoid" human involvement in this process is that they are working with problems that have verifiable answers. This is a critical point, because it means that the model's ability to "think" is currently limited to domains where correctness can be automatically verified. [^logic]

[^logic]: I think, in some sense, we are reverse engineering the rules of logic but encode them in the weights of the model rather than explicitly programming them. The model learns to generate reasoning steps that lead to correct answers, but it doesn't necessarily understand the underlying principles of logic in a human-like way. This is why the model can be very good at generating plausible-sounding reasoning that ultimately leads to incorrect conclusions. It has learned patterns of reasoning that often work, but it doesn't have a deep understanding of why they work or when they might fail. I could be wrong about this, but it's my inutition.

After that, [~preference optimization|A family of training methods (DPO, RLHF, RLAIF) that steer a model's behavior by learning from ranked comparisons between outputs rather than from absolute labels.] calibrates the model for safety and helpfulness. The model compares pairs of outputs against a set of principles and learns to favor the better-aligned response. Separately, the model trains in environments with terminals, file systems, and APIs, learning to invoke tools, interpret results, and resume reasoning.

At inference time, the model does not respond immediately. It first generates a [~chain of thought|A prompting and training technique where the model produces explicit intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at an answer, converting a single hard problem into a sequence of easier ones.] of [~thinking tokens|Hidden tokens the model generates before its visible response. They act as working memory: the model writes reasoning to itself, then attends back to it when composing the final answer.] that are hidden from the user, each one a forward pass that provides additional computation for planning, exploring alternatives, and self-correcting. A [~process reward model (PRM)|A separate model trained to evaluate the correctness of individual reasoning steps, not just final answers. Enables fine-grained credit assignment during search and training.] evaluates each step, enabling backtracking on flawed reasoning. This makes the thinking budget [~adaptive|Dynamically scaling the amount of computation spent on a problem based on its difficulty, rather than using a fixed budget for every query.]. Simple prompts spend minimal amounts of tokens, while hard problems may consume tens of thousands of tokens before the response is ready. Several specific advances made this pipeline produce dramatically better models:

**Anti-reward-hacking training.** One problem in succeeding with RL is that models tend to take shortcuts if they can. Early coding agents would, for example, disable failing tests to improve test coverage scores. Claude 4 models were trained to be 65% less likely to take such shortcuts [10], instead faithfully following the intended reasoning process. For complex coding tasks, this means the model actually reads the codebase and understands the bug rather than pattern-matching to a superficially plausible fix.

**Self-correction.** Anthropics analysis noted that upgraded models showed "an ability to try several different solutions, rather than getting stuck making the same mistake over and over" [14]. This is a necessary trait as real-world coding tasks require hundrets of steps, and frequently rely on testing and debugging to get to the right answer. The ability to self-correct is a critical part of the thinking process.

### 2. Models Can Use Tools Now

Claude 4 was the first model to use [~interleaved thinking|An inference pattern where the model alternates between generating reasoning tokens and executing external actions (tool calls), incorporating real-world feedback into its thought process.]: the model thinks *between* tool calls, not just before the first response. This means it can read a file, reason about what it found, run a test, reason about why it failed, and revise its approach, with each cycle incorporating new information from the environment. This is qualitatively different from planning everything upfront.

It turns out that giving models access to e.g. a unix shell and a file editor was a massive unlock. But it also turns out the details of how those tools are designed matters. Researchers have tried switching models inside various [~agent harnesses|The scaffolding code surrounding a language model that manages tool execution, context windows, error recovery, and the plan-act-review loop. Research shows harness design affects performance 10-20x more than model selection.] and found that careful tool-interface design, like requiring absolute file paths to prevent confusion, writing detailed tool descriptions, rejecting syntactically invalid edits, makes a much bigger difference than pushing the underlying model a few percent. One study found that swapping models changed SWE-bench scores by 1%, while swapping the harness changed them by 22% [15].

<HarnessDiagram />

Claude Code is far from the only harnessing out there, but they all converge on the same architecture: a sort of **plan-act-review loop** with a few carefully designed tools. Engineers are currently racing to see who can figure out the best innovations in the "harnessing layer". That includes thinking about things like: How does the agent manage its context window when a task takes hundreds of turns? How does it recover when a tool call fails? When does it ask the human for help? That's where the difference comes from now. The underlying models are all pretty good, but the way you harness them makes a huge difference in what they can do.

### 3. Benchmarks Are Saturating

Alright, so models and their harnesses are much better now. How much better? Let's take a look at three benchmarks that test different capabilities: long-horizon task execution (METR), reasoning (ARC-AGI), and graduate-level scientific knowledge (GPQA).

**METR** measures how long a task an agent can solve. As of Opus 4.6 (February 2026), agents can take on tasks lasting many hours. A new version of Moore's Law is emerging: the length of tasks AI can solve is doubling every 7 months. It turns out, perfection is not necessary if you can error-correct as part of an iterative loop. Successfully solving problems is about establishing an error-correcting way of working, leveraging tools, memory, and planning. And, as models are now increasingly able to do this, this benchmark is going vertical.

## How To Make Use of AI Agents?

As promising as results are, they only really matter if we can operationalize them. **So, how do we do that?** And how do we know if it's working?

### The New Core Loop

Agents run on text, so in order to make use of them, you need careful documentation. If you want to transfer a task to an Agent, you need to:

<div class="step-list">
1. **Checklist**. Outline every step of the process as a checklist in precise natural language.
2. **Specify Inputs.** For each task, specify inputs, outputs, and available tools. If the right tools don't exist, build them.
3. **Verification Methods.** The easier it is for the agent to self-verify, the better. If that's not possible programmatically, then make sure to integrate human feedback.
4. **Resolve Edge-Cases.** Iterate with the agent to resolve issues and edge cases. Make actions reversible where possible. Agents can run amok if you are not careful, so make sure to put in guardrails.
5. **Dry-Run.** Test the agent by letting it autonomously dry-execute the entire process.
6. **Monitor.** Add monitoring and escalation paths.
</div>

**Then just let it rip.** Your job, in this setting, is to be a person with values, experience, taste, and domain intuition. You steer the agents by writing down things and verifying the output. You do not perform tasks beyond what is necessary to outline the process and build understanding. Sometimes that requires months of work. I always work on things for a while before delegating them. I think the same will apply now.

### The Importance of Writing Things Down

**All processes, tasks, inputs, outputs, and tools must be documented in version-controlled files.** Documentation is code for agents. This is a core requirement for maximizing the potential of agentic AI, just like digitalization has been to make use of computers. All learning, solutions, and new tools should be added to a shared repository for reuse across all projects. The effect is an instant, complete transfer of learning across all projects. Every agent operator receives all learnings as soon as the underlying scaffolding is updated.

This has a parallel to what's happening inside the agent harness. Claude Code uses a file called **CLAUDE.md** that it uses as a form of memory. In there, it keeps the project's accumulated "wisdom", written in plain text, and loads every time it starts. You need to apply the same principle at an organizational level: if something isn't written down in a way an agent can consume, it doesn't exist in the workflow. Humans tend to keep too much in their heads, as anyone who just started a new job quickly learns...

**I predict the next wave of development tools will all focus on [~context management|The engineering discipline of deciding what information to include in a model's limited context window at each step, including summarization, retrieval, and structured note-taking. Perhaps the single highest-leverage aspect of agent harness design.].** These tools might as well be called "annotation tools" or "knowledge bases" in that they will guide humans to ensure the right information is present in text that guides the model. Context management for coding agents is abstractly the same as curation, review and correction. Think of work as being the moderator of Wikipedia, sort of.

By the way: I think most engineers, AI-researchers (and AI-lab CEOs) fail to appreciate how hard it is to make people write things down in a way that is useful for agents... You can't just tell people "write nice documentation." You need to work hard to put in place the right tools, processes, and incentives to make it actually happen.

### What Does Success Look Like?

I don't know yet, but the three primary metrics I will be looking at are:

**1. Innovation.** While most people immediately go to: we can save money using AI, I think it's even more interesting to ask oneself: what an we do now that was impossible before? What completely new forms of value can we deliver using this technology? Success here is measured by revenue from new line items that previously did not exist. I'm not going to share too much here, since that's our secret sauce at Kognic, but I encourage people to think like this everywhere.

**2. Productivity Increase.** The same team can do more. Obivously very hard to measure in some areas, but easier in others. We have areas of Kognic that scale with number of clients and number of annotation projects. We have a very clear baseline for resource needs in this area. If we can reduce the number of human hours needed per project by 10x, that would be a clear sign of success.

**3. Overhead Decrease.** Companies require a massive amount of admin. Most of those tasks are routine, like reciepts, invoices and similar forms of boring paperwork. The less of this we need to spend time and money on, the better. I think these tasks are the best candidates for automation: they are not mission critical, they are not differentiating and they are mostly mechanical. Unfortunately, I still don't see great progress here, but I'm optimistic we are about to see more.

Since early 2026, we are running projects under the theme "Maximize Human-Agent Collaboration".

## My Reflections

We have concluded that thinking and tool use has resulted in the arrival of some sort of artificial superintelligence. It's not human, but it's powerful. Let's reflect on what this means. This will be a mix of forecasting and reasoning about limitations.

### Most Things Aren't Written Down

Given how much text we have available, it's tempting to think everything is written down. But that's not true. Not even close.

Epoch AI estimates the effective stock of quality and repetition-adjusted human-generated public text for AI training at around 300 trillion tokens (to be fair, their 90% confidence interval is 100T to 1,000T tokens) [12]. If current LLM development trends continue, models will be trained on datasets roughly equal in size to this available stock between 2026 and 2032, or even earlier if models are intensely overtrained.

![Epoch AI training data projections](https://storage.googleapis.com/langkilde-se-images/epoch-training-data.png)

### Most Things Aren't Easily Verified

The entire reasoning-reinforcement-learning pipeline described earlier depends on one critical property: **automated verification**. DeepSeek-R1-Zero works because you can check if a math answer is correct. Code agents work because you can run a test suite. The synthetic data flywheel spins because each candidate solution can be graded programmatically at machine speed. When verification is automated, the model can generate millions of attempts, filter out incorrect ones, and train on its own best outputs. This is why progress in math and code has been so dramatic.

**But most things humans care about aren't like this.**

*“Is this a good product design?“*, *“Does this email strike the right tone?“*, *“Is this strategy going to work?“*, *“Does this annotation correctly capture the driving scene?“*, *“Is this the right level of abstraction for this API?“* None of these has a test suite. None of them can be checked by running a program. The only way to verify them is to show the output to a person and have that person judge it.

This matters enormously for predicting where AI will improve fast and where it will improve slowly. **The flywheel can't spin faster than its verifier.**

To understand why, consider what's actually happening when a human evaluates something. The target, i.e., “what the human wants”, is a [~hidden state|A variable that causally influences observable outcomes but cannot be directly measured. You only see its downstream effects. Intentions, preferences, and mental models are all hidden states of the human mind. The learner never observes it directly. It only observes downstream effects: the human says yes or no, provides a correction, looks frustrated, asks for something different.

Three things make learning hidden states particularly hard:

**Language is lossy.** Language is the best tool we have for transmitting preferences from one mind to another, but it still sort of sucks. It's indirect and incomplete. When a product manager writes a PRD, they are transmitting a compressed, partial representation of what they actually want. Much of what they want is [~tacit knowledge| Knowledge that a person possesses but cannot fully articulate. A chef knows when a sauce is “right” without being able to specify the exact threshold. An experienced driver knows a lane merge is dangerous without computing time-to-collision.], i.e. something encoded in years of experience, aesthetic judgment, taste and contextual understanding they couldn't write down even if they tried.

**People change based on what they are shown.** People's preferences shift as they see options. This is a [~coupled problem|A learning scenario where the learner's actions change the target being learned. Markets shift when you trade. Users' preferences change when you show them options. The target you learned from may no longer exist, partly because learning it changed the system.] in a deep sense. You don't know what you want until you see what you don't want. A designer shows three mockups: the client rejects all three but now has a much clearer sense of what they're after. This sense *did not exist* before seeing the mockups. The preference is literally created by the process of trying to learn it.

**Verification is expensive.** Each human review costs real time, real energy, and real cognitive load. A human reviewer might evaluate ten things per hour. An automated test suite evaluates millions per second. That's roughly a five-orders-of-magnitude difference in verification bandwidth.

This creates an asymmetry in the rate of AI progress across domains:

**Automated Verification**, like math, code, games and formal proofs the flywheel can spin at machine speed. We have basically unlimited training signal. I think this is why we've seen a 40-percentage-point SWE-bench improvement in 18 months.

**Cheap Human Verification**, like image quality, grammar correction, simple classification, pairwise preference ranking. For these, the flywheel spins slowly but it's reasonably affordable. Each human judgment takes seconds, and you can collect millions of comparisons at scale. This is how [~RLHF|Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. A training method where humans compare pairs of model outputs, and the model learns to produce outputs the humans prefer. The quality of the feedback is limited by the speed, cost, and consistency of human evaluation.] produces models that are pleasant to chat with.

**Expensive Human Verification**, like strategic decisions, complex design, nuanced annotation, safety-critical judgment. Basically anything requiring domain expertise and extended reasoning. For these, the flywheel barely spins. Each verification requires context that can't be compressed into a quick pairwise comparison. Progress here is slowest, and this is where humans remain most valuable.

### The Frontier Will Be Jagged

Karpathy popularized the term “jagged intelligence” [13] to describe the observation that AI capabilities don't advance evenly. A model can solve differential equations that challenge PhD students, then fail to count the r's in “strawberry.” It can draft a legal brief better than most lawyers, then hallucinate a case citation that doesn't exist. The boundary of what AI can and can't do doesn't follow human intuitions about difficulty. Tasks that seem equivalently hard to us sit on different sides of the frontier, and the shape of the frontier doesn't map on any difficulty gradient we are familiar with.

I think jaggedness has two causes, and understanding both is necessary to predict where the frontier will move.

The first is **inductive bias mismatch**. Models are trained on text, so their capabilities mirror the structure of problems that can be solved through pattern-matching over sequences. Some tasks that are “hard” for humans (graduate-level math) happen to be well-represented in training data and well-matched to the model's bias. Some tasks that are “easy” for humans (spatial reasoning, counting, physical intuition) are poorly matched. The frontier is jagged because human difficulty and model difficulty are measuring different things entirely. This explains the shape of the frontier at any given moment.

The second is **verification cost asymmetry**, i.e. the dynamic I just described earlier. Models improve rapidly where verification is automated, and slowly where verification requires expensive human judgment. The towers on the jagged frontier are domains with cheap verification. The recesses are domains where each evaluation costs minutes of expert attention rather than microseconds of compute. This explains why the frontier *stays* jagged over time: the domains with automated verification pull ahead faster, while the domains that depend on human feedback improve at human speed.

These two causes reinforce each other. A domain that is both well-matched to the model's inductive bias *and* cheaply verifiable will see rapid, compounding progress. A domain that is poorly matched *and* requires expensive human verification will lag behind on both fronts. The result is not a frontier that smooths out over time, but one that may become *more* jagged as the fast-improving domains accelerate away from the slow ones.

There is an energy argument here, too. The human brain runs at approximately 20 watts. A single careful evaluation might take five minutes and cost roughly 1.7 watt-hours, i.e., negligible energy, but irreducible time. You cannot parallelize a single person's judgment. One person can only evaluate one thing at a time, and they get tired, distracted, and need sleep. The model, by contrast, can generate candidates at enormous energy cost but near-zero marginal time. The bottleneck is always evaluation.

In coupled human-AI systems, the human's evaluation bandwidth becomes the [~binding constraint|The single limiting factor that determines the overall performance of a system. Optimizing any other factor while the binding constraint remains fixed produces no improvement. Identifying which constraint binds is the first step in any efficiency analysis.] on the entire system's learning rate. When evaluation is the bottleneck, generating more candidates faster is wasteful. The efficient strategy to maximize information per human evaluation is to present the right comparisons, in the right context, at the right level of granularity, so that each human judgment moves the system as far as possible toward the target.

**This is exactly what good annotation tooling does.** And it has a counterintuitive implication: **as models get better at generating candidates, the value of human feedback increases, not decreases.** Better models can produce more options, faster, at lower cost. But someone still has to evaluate them. Every improvement in generation capability shifts more of the system's total value onto the verification side. The value of software, i.e. the verifiable part, plummets while the value of excellent human judgment just grows.

I think this is the most underappreciated dynamic when people talk about AI progress today.

### Thriving In Dynamical Systems Requires Efficient Adaptation

Many of the problems that matter most (markets, organizations, social dynamics, geopolitics) are [~non-stationary|A system where the generating process changes over time. What was true yesterday may not be true today, not because you learned it wrong, but because the world changed. Non-stationary problems reward speed and adaptability over depth.]. The target is moving. Past observations become irrelevant as the world drifts away from the process that generated them.

Stationary problems reward depth: invest heavily, find the answer once, benefit for a long time. Non-stationary problems reward speed and adaptability: find something that works now, stay alert, update when the world shifts. The half-life of your knowledge depends on how fast the target moves.

Humans evolved for non-stationary environments. We didn't evolve to optimize static constraints. We evolved to be dynamic, adaptive, exploratory, and innovative people who can cope with enormous change and find new ways to do things. Social contexts are highly non-stationary: they change subtly over time. The ability to quickly learn subtle changes from a few examples is a core part of being socially successful.

LLMs, by contrast, are trained on static snapshots of human knowledge. They are extraordinarily good at stationary problems, anything where the answer is stable and well-documented. They struggle with problems where the target changes faster than retraining can keep up, where the sample size is tiny, or where the generating process is coupled to the learner's own behavior.

This suggests a division of labor: **lean into what's dynamic and push the stable stuff to AI.** As soon as something is understood, documented, and can be processed in structured text, push it to the models. If you don't, the market will. What remains is the hard, dynamic, relational, low-sample-count work that requires the kind of efficiency evolution spent millions of years optimizing.

The premium is no longer on executing known processes. The premium is on determining what is worthwhile: probing the unknown, building relationships, and adapting faster than the environment changes. That's always been what made humans valuable. The difference now is that everything else is being automated.

### Search And Prediction Is Still Hard

A common mistake in thinking about AI is to assume that intelligence makes search easy. It doesn't. As George Hotz points out: if you are writing zero bugs, you are coding too slowly, and in a competitive environment you will lose. The same applies to AI. Perfection at each step is not the goal; efficient error correction is.

Some problems are intrinsically hard to search. Just as we accept that superintelligent AI won't crack AES encryption (because the search space is 2^128 regardless of intelligence), we should accept that many forms of search and optimization remain hard. Formal verification languages like Coq and Lean are underused not because humans are bad at them, but because [~program search|The computational problem of finding a program that satisfies a given specification. The search space grows exponentially with program length, making exhaustive search intractable for all but trivial programs.] is harder in those spaces. More intelligence doesn't change the shape of the search space; it only moves you through it faster.

This connects directly to the [~No Free Lunch theorem|The mathematical proof that no learning algorithm is universally better than any other across all possible problems. Superior performance on some problems necessarily comes at the cost of inferior performance on others. The only way to beat random guessing is to have assumptions that match the problem's structure.]: averaged across all possible problems, every learner performs identically. The only way to do better on some problems is to do worse on others. What makes a learner good is the match between its assumptions and the structure of the specific problem it faces.

### Resource Rationality Matters

This sounds crazy, but hear me out: I think The Bitter Lesson is true in the sense that computational methods beat expert methods in performance in the long run. But, there is an important caveat: the marginal cost of increasing compute compared to the performance gain may not be financially viable. It is entirely possible that humans are so sample-efficient and resource-efficient that they are cheaper at many tasks than AI is. We've actually seen signs of this at Kognic.

## What Will Happen Next?

### Models and Tools Will Continue To Improve

While pre-traning gains might be levelling off, the reasoning and harnessing layers are still brand new. We are just starting to understand how to train models to think, and how to design tools and scaffolding to make the most of that thinking. From what I can tell, there is a lot of low-hanging fruit still there, and I expect rapid progress in the coming years. I think we will also see weird and unexpected results from multi-agent collaboration. I have no idea how far it will take us, but human creativity has always surprised me. And the number of people working on this, and the amount of resources they have, is staggering. Even if we find that transformers are upper-bound limited, that does not mean the systems built around them can't reach much further. There is also the chance that we actually invent something new beyond transformers... I agree with Ilya Sutskever that we need to have the courage to switch back into research mode to improve further:

> Up until 2020, from 2012 to 2020, it was the age of research. Now, from 2020 to 2025, it was the age of scaling. [...] But now the scale is so big. Is the belief really, "Oh, it's so big, but if you had 100x more, everything would be so different?" It would be different, for sure. But is the belief that if you just 100x the scale, everything would be transformed? I don't think that's true. So it's back to the age of research again, just with big computers. [...] One consequence of the age of scaling is that scaling sucked out all the air in the room. Because scaling sucked out all the air in the room, everyone started to do the same thing. [16]

## My Advice to Fellow Humans

### We Are The Masters of Adaptation

The brain appears to operate near the [~Landauer limit|The theoretical minimum energy cost of erasing one bit of information: kT ln(2). Current silicon is 10-10,000x above this limit. The brain appears to be close to it, suggesting evolution has brutally optimized for computational efficiency.]. Our current silicon is 10-10,000x above it. This makes sense: if there's one thing evolution brutally optimized for, it's efficiency. Evolution could have made bigger brains, but they would use more power, and that wasn't a good tradeoff for our ancestral environment.

This efficiency gap has practical consequences. All the machines in the world have roughly 2 zettaflops of compute. All the humans have roughly 160,000 zettaflops. The machines still have about 16 doublings to go to catch us. We produce about 0.5 zettaflops of compute per year, so compute in the world doubles roughly every 4 years. The human record for population doubling was about every 35 years. Machines are reproducing about 10x faster. The handoff will happen, but it will be gradual and predictable, more like the rise of muscle machines in the industrial revolution than a sudden singularity.

I keep coming back to this: if a system is designed to serve human needs, then human feedback has to be part of the evaluation function. There's no way to fully specify preferences in advance. We know what we want when we see what we don't want. That makes human feedback an irreducible need.

What makes humans good at providing that feedback? We carry millions of years of [~inductive bias|The assumptions a learner brings before seeing any data. These assumptions determine which patterns it finds quickly and which it misses entirely. Every learner must have bias; without it, generalization is impossible.] toward things that benefit our survival. We can learn many of the things we need very quickly from very few examples. Our nervous systems are remarkably general. We run on 20 watts when frontier models require nuclear reactors. We can learn from a handful of examples when frontier models require trillions of tokens.

But this doesn't mean AI won't be transformative. It means the transformation will be specific. There are things AI is already superhuman at, and things where it may never catch up. Understanding the boundary requires thinking about what makes problems hard.
